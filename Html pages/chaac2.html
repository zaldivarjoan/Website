The Mayans agricultural practices and rituals are also a key factor particularly their use of almanacs to predict the weather and its impact to their crops. The chacs, both good and negative, who are said to regulate the weather are described in these almanacs. The almanacs mention the many gifts presented to the chacs, such as corn and other sorts of animals. The Book of Chilam Balam has allusions to agricultural rites as well as calendars that predict good and bad days for each month of the year. Although the glyphic inscriptions of the Dresden Codex are written in complicated, generally unclear language, it is clear that the ancient. Deities possessed both benevolent and malevolent qualities, notably the chacs and balams, which are both linked with the field and bush, as well as fertility.

The persistence and change of agricultural rituals in the Yucatan. Agricultural rites were practiced on the Yucatán Peninsula during the Colonial Period, despite a scarcity of evidence in sixteenth-century sources. In an 1813 account, according to the book, Granado Baeza, a parish priest in Yaxcabá, gives insight into the agricultural rites during the Colonial Period. Baeza describes a ceremony called "tich" or "cornfield mass" in this report, which was an imitation of the real mass and included elements like building an altar table, preparing ceremonial masa, sacrificing a turkey, invoking certain saints and the pahauteas, offering to the world directions, and feasting. The Baeza's report is crucial because it demonstrates that the ritual in 1813 is very similar to the chha chac ceremony that is still conducted today, with common features enduring across time. The only reading that I can compare this part of the book to is Día de Los Muertos where there is basically a ceremony with set practices for people. Overall I’ve learned that chaac or more known as chac takes various forms and plays an important role to the Mayans and their lifestyle which will either benefit them or not.
